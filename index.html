Explorer for Investing in AI-enabled Services

This dashboard provides an overview for priority sub-verticals to invest in across various professional services, combining data on AI augmentation on the industry, importance of human elements / judgement, as well as industry fundamentals

Key Takeaway
  • Healthcare Services as an industry is a major beneficiary of AI, due to AI's benefits to the providers' day-to-day activities, importance of continued human involvement, as well as fundamental industry demand
  • The data confirms some of the industries that early pioneers of AI roll-up strategies are focused on, for example IT Services (vis-a-vis Information Security Analysts), Tax Firms (vis-a-vis Tax Preparers)
  • It is important to invest in sub categories where humans have to apply judgement vs. performing administrative tasks, even though they might fall under the same general industry. For example, Human Resource Specialists (i.e., recruiters) are expected to benefit from AI while the impact for Human Resource Assistants is more muted, as HR assistants perform more administrative tasks
  • There are many facets / tasks beyond what we typically associate an occupation with. For example, athletic trainers are expected to benefit from AI: even though we typically think of their job as physically training athletes, a meaningful portion of their jobs is related to developing training regimes independently and preparing administrative reports
  • Certain industries are more resilient to AI than one might expect because of the importance of being accurate / high cost of failure, even though there might be secular headwind for those industries (e.g., Interpreters and Translators, Travel Agents)
  • More occupations have reported augmentation usage for AI as opposed to automation usage to date, with majority of the occupations reporting no meaningful AI usage
Limitation of the Dashboard
  • The view for industries to invest in is based on data at the occupation level, and the exact sub-sectors to invest in might differ from what is listed under "Most Relevant Industry", which is based on matching NAICS with occupation code (e.g., IT Services might be the most investable sub-sector associated with Information Security Analyst, vs. broader industry for Computer System Design Services as suggested by the dashboard)
  • The industry attractiveness scorecard does not account for recession resilience. For example, real estate brokerage scores well for this dashboard even though its revenue source is quite cyclical
  • The industry attractiveness scorecard does not account for fragmentation of the market. For example, insurance sales agents / brokerage industry scores well for this dashboard even though the industry is somewhat consolidated after active PE activities in the last 10 years
  • The underlying AI usage data is inferred from task-level AI interaction classifications, so some prompts may reflect personal use rather than on-the-job use and can still be mapped to a professional occupation
  • AI usage is likely going to evolve as capabilities of the model improve; usage data in the dashboard is based on Anthropic published data on Claude chat usage in February 2026

Methodology

This dashboard combines occupation-level AI task usage patterns, O*NET task and work-context measures, and industry mapping and growth data to show where AI appears more likely to augment work, where displacement risk remains, and which occupation-led service verticals look most investable.

AI Augmentation vs. Automation

This tab estimates whether currently observed AI usage patterns for an occupation lean more toward augmenting work or automating work.

Task-level AI score

Each AI-exposed task receives a net score based on augmentation share minus automation share, scaled by O*NET task importance.

task_score = 100 x (augmentation_share - automation_share) x (importance - 1) / 4

Directive and Feedback Loop are grouped as automation. Task Iteration, Validation, and Learning are grouped as augmentation.

automation_share = Directive + Feedback Loop
augmentation_share = Task Iteration + Validation + Learning

Human / physical task filter

Tasks that still require embodied, in-person, hands-on, equipment-based, or live-service execution are treated as human / physical tasks. Those tasks remain visible in the drill-down, but their displayed AI score is forced to 0 so the occupation score focuses on tasks that current AI use can more plausibly reshape.

The current implementation uses a model-based classifier (Haiku 4.5) that reads each O*NET task statement against an operational rubric. A task is treated as human / physical if completing it end-to-end requires manipulating physical objects, materials, tools, machinery, or vehicles; touching, examining, or moving a person or animal; being bodily present at a specific work location; or live face-to-face presence where the embodied interaction is part of the work. Tasks that can plausibly be accomplished through writing, reading, analysis, software operation, or remote communication (phone, video, chat, email) are not treated as human / physical, even when the verbs sound hands-on (for example, "operate" software, "prepare" reports, or "interview" by telephone).

Occupation-level AI score

The occupation score is the frequency-weighted sum of score-eligible AI tasks, using each task’s O*NET frequency weight share in the occupation.

occupation_ai_score = Sum(task_score x task_frequency_weight_share)

A task contributes to the occupation score only if it is AI-exposed, not human / physical, has classified AI interaction data, and has a non-zero displayed task score.

Coverage floor and sparse-task rule

To avoid overinterpreting tiny pockets of AI exposure, the dashboard sets the occupation AI score to 0 when either condition below is true:

if eligible_ai_task_count <= 1, occupation_ai_score = 0
if eligible_ai_task_coverage < 10%, occupation_ai_score = 0

Here, eligible AI task coverage means score-eligible AI tasks divided by total occupation tasks.

AI Resilience

This tab shows the four O*NET work-context measures used in the dashboard’s resilience lens.

AI_resilience = Average(Contact With Others,
Consequence of Error,
Impact of Decisions on Co-workers or Company Results,
Importance of Being Exact or Accurate)

Higher values indicate occupations with stronger interpersonal, precision, or consequence-sensitive work-context features that may be harder to fully substitute away.

AI vs. Work Context

This tab plots each occupation using two scores already built elsewhere in the dashboard: the occupation AI augmentation-vs.-automation score on the x-axis and AI Resilience on the y-axis.

x-axis = occupation_ai_score
y-axis = AI_resilience

The top-benefit and top-suffer lists use the sum of standardized z-scores across those two axes.

combined_z = z(ai_score) + z(AI_resilience)

Higher combined z-scores indicate stronger combined upside across both dimensions. Lower combined z-scores indicate more combined downside.

onetcenter.org/dictionary/30.2/excel/work_context.html
Industry Data
Industry employment and wage reference tables used to support the industry mapping and growth fields shown in the dashboard.
bls.gov/oes/tables.htm

All scores in this dashboard reflect observed current usage patterns, structural job characteristics, and industry reference data in the loaded datasets, not a forecast of ultimate AI capability or guaranteed job outcomes.
